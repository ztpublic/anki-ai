**The Dragon Under China**

Long, long ago, before there were cities, roads, or people, a giant stone dragon slept under the land we now call China.

Its **head** rested high in the west, its **body** stretched across the middle, and its **tail** reached toward the eastern sea.

One day, the dragon began to move.

In the far southwest, a huge land called **India** slowly pushed northward and bumped into Asia. The dragon’s head wrinkled and rose upward. Those wrinkles became the **Himalayas** and the **Tibetan Plateau**. That is why western China is so high. It is often called the “Roof of the World.”

The dragon’s back was not smooth. Some places were squeezed into **mountains**, while other places bent downward into **basins**. In the northwest, places like the **Tarim Basin** and **Junggar Basin** formed like giant bowls between mountain ranges. Sand and dust gathered there, and over time some areas became deserts, such as the **Taklamakan Desert**.

As the dragon stretched eastward, rivers began to flow off the high land. They carried broken pieces of mountain—sand, mud, and gravel—across the country. These pieces piled up in plains and valleys. That is how many lower lands in central and eastern China were built.

In the north, the wind joined the story. For thousands of years, wind blew fine yellow dust and dropped it across the land. Layer upon layer, it formed the **Loess Plateau**. The soil there is soft and fertile, but it can also erode easily when water cuts through it.

In central China, old mountains and stable blocks of crust helped shape the land. The **Qinling Mountains** became an important dividing line between north and south China. To the west of the middle lands, the **Sichuan Basin** sat like a deep, quiet bowl surrounded by mountains.

In the south, rainwater whispered to the rocks. It slowly dissolved limestone and carved strange towers, caves, and underground rivers. This made the famous **karst landscapes** of places like **Guilin**. So while the west rose high and rough, the south became a land of stone forests and misty peaks.

In the east, the dragon’s tail reached the sea. There, rivers such as the **Yellow River** and the **Yangtze River** spread out sediments and helped build wide plains and deltas. These flatter lands became good places for farming and large settlements.

Deep below all of this were very old pieces of Earth’s crust, like the **North China Craton** and the **South China Block**. These ancient foundations were like the dragon’s bones. They are much older than the Himalayas and tell geologists that China is made from several crustal pieces joined together over a very long time.

So if you look at China today, you can read the dragon’s body:

* **High mountains and plateau in the west** from continental collision
* **Basins and deserts in the northwest** where crust bent and sediments collected
* **Dust-covered plateau in the north** shaped by wind
* **Karst hills and caves in the south** shaped by dissolving limestone
* **Broad plains and river deltas in the east** built by sediments from great rivers

And that is the geology of China:

A land built by **collision, uplift, wind, water, and deep time**.
